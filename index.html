Mathematical Theory of Spatiotemporal Detection of the THz Electric Field

From the mechanical displacement of an optical delay line to the reconstruction of \(\mathbf E(x,y,t)\), its spectrum \(\widetilde{\mathbf E}(x,y,\omega)\), local arrival time, wavefront, and frequency-resolved polarization.

Scope. This page develops the measurement as a linear sampling problem. It begins with the relation between translation-stage displacement and optical delay, derives the electro-optic phase retardation, establishes the detector transfer function, and then explains—frame by frame—how the temporal evolution of a broadband THz field is reconstructed across a two-dimensional plane. No experimental procedure or code is included.

0. Fourier and sign conventions

Throughout this document, the temporal Fourier transform is defined by

\[\widetilde E(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}E(t)e^{+i\omega t}\,dt,\qquad E(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\widetilde E(\omega)e^{-i\omega t}\,d\omega.\tag{1}\]

For a real electric field, \(\widetilde E(-\omega)=\widetilde E^*(\omega)\). A delayed waveform \(E(t-t_0)\) has spectrum \(\widetilde E(\omega)e^{+i\omega t_0}\); therefore, with this convention, a positive time delay produces a positive linear spectral-phase slope.

Vector notation
The transverse field is \(\mathbf E_\perp=E_x\hat{\mathbf x}+E_y\hat{\mathbf y}\). A scalar EO measurement records one projection \(E_q=\hat{\mathbf q}\cdot\mathbf E_\perp\), where \(\hat{\mathbf q}\) is fixed by the crystal and analyzer geometry.

1. Delay line: converting displacement into time

Electro-optic sampling does not directly use an electronic oscilloscope fast enough to follow a sub-picosecond THz transient. Instead, an ultrashort optical probe pulse acts as a movable temporal gate. The relative pump–probe delay is controlled by translating a retroreflector.

1.1 Optical path and delay

Let \(x\) be the physical translation of a retroreflector in the probe arm. The optical beam travels to the retroreflector and back, so the path length changes by \(2x\). The relative delay is therefore

\[\tau(x)=\tau_0+s\frac{2x}{c},\tag{2}\]

where \(c\) is the speed of light, \(\tau_0\) is an arbitrary reference delay, and \(s=+1\) or \(-1\) depends on which arm is moved and on the chosen delay convention. The magnitude of the conversion is always

\[\left|\frac{d\tau}{dx}\right|=\frac{2}{c}=6.67128\ \mathrm{fs\,\mu m^{-1}}.\tag{3}\]

Thus, a \(10~\mu\mathrm m\) stage step corresponds to \(66.7~\mathrm{fs}\) of temporal sampling. The delay line is therefore a calibrated space-to-time converter.

1.2 What the delay coordinate means

At delay \(\tau\), the probe gate overlaps the THz field near the laboratory time \(t=\tau\). Scanning \(\tau\) does not slow down a single pulse. Instead, the experiment repeats the same phase-locked event many times and samples a different temporal slice on each repetition. The reconstructed trace is valid only when the THz transient is reproducible and synchronized to the probe.

Sampling interpretation
For an ideal delta-function gate, the measured signal at delay \(\tau\) is proportional to \(E_{\mathrm{THz}}(\tau)\). For a finite optical gate, the signal is a convolution with the probe intensity envelope and the EO-crystal response.

2. Discrete temporal sampling and spectral limits

For an equally spaced delay scan,

\[x_n=x_0+n\Delta x,\qquad t_n=t_0+n\Delta t,\qquad \Delta t=\frac{2\Delta x}{c},\quad n=0,1,\dots,N-1.\tag{4}\]

The discrete time window and the fundamental Fourier grid are

\[T_{\mathrm{DFT}}=N\Delta t,\qquad \Delta f=\frac{1}{N\Delta t},\qquad f_m=\frac{m}{N\Delta t}.\tag{5}\]

The largest frequency that can be represented without aliasing is the Nyquist frequency

\[f_{\mathrm N}=\frac{1}{2\Delta t}=\frac{c}{4\Delta x}.\tag{6}\]

Equations (5) and (6) express two independent design constraints: the delay step controls the maximum measurable frequency, whereas the total delay window controls the true spectral resolution. Zero padding can interpolate the plotted spectrum but cannot improve \(\Delta f\).

Finite-window consequence. Multiplication by a temporal window \(w(t)\) becomes convolution in frequency: \(\widetilde E_{\mathrm{win}}(\omega)=\widetilde w(\omega)*\widetilde E(\omega)\). Spectral leakage is therefore a mathematical consequence of truncating the waveform, not merely a plotting artifact.

3. Why a THz transient is commonly bipolar

The radiated far field from a localized transient current is proportional, under the radiation-zone approximation, to the time derivative of the transverse current evaluated at the retarded time:

\[\mathbf E_{\mathrm{rad}}(\mathbf r,t)\propto \frac{1}{R}\frac{\partial \mathbf J_\perp}{\partial t}\!\left(t-\frac{R}{c}\right).\tag{7}\]

A current that rises and then decays therefore produces opposite-polarity field lobes. A convenient analytical model is a Gaussian current transient,

\[J(t)=J_0e^{-t^2/(2\sigma^2)},\qquad E(t)=E_0\frac{t}{\sigma}e^{-t^2/(2\sigma^2)}.\tag{8}\]

The field has zero time integral, a zero crossing at \(t=0\), and extrema at \(t=\pm\sigma\). Its spectrum is proportional to

\[\widetilde E(\omega)\propto i\omega\,e^{-\omega^2\sigma^2/2},\tag{9}\]

which vanishes at DC and emphasizes a broad band of finite frequencies. Equation (8) is an illustrative single-cycle model; the actual waveform is determined by the source current, propagation, interfaces, material dispersion, and detector response.

4. Electro-optic interaction in the detector crystal

4.1 Pockels perturbation of the dielectric impermeability

In a linear electro-optic crystal, the THz field perturbs the optical impermeability tensor \(\boldsymbol\eta=\boldsymbol\varepsilon_r^{-1}\):

\[\Delta\eta_{ij}(t)=\sum_k r_{ijk}E_k^{\mathrm{THz}}(t),\tag{10}\]

where \(r_{ijk}\) is the Pockels tensor. After transforming to the principal axes selected by the crystal orientation, probe polarization, and THz polarization, the two optical eigenindices are, to first order,

\[n_{\pm}\simeq n_o\mp\frac{1}{2}n_o^3r_{\mathrm{eff}}E_q^{\mathrm{THz}},\qquad \Delta n=n_- - n_+=n_o^3r_{\mathrm{eff}}E_q^{\mathrm{THz}}.\tag{11}\]

Here \(r_{\mathrm{eff}}\) includes the tensor projection and may be frequency dependent. The THz field therefore converts the EO crystal into a transient wave plate.

4.2 Thin-crystal, instantaneous-gate limit

If the THz field is uniform over a crystal of thickness \(L\), phase matched to the probe, and sampled by an infinitesimally short gate, the induced retardation is

\[\Gamma(\tau)=\frac{2\pi L}{\lambda_0}\Delta n(\tau) =\frac{2\pi L}{\lambda_0}n_o^3r_{\mathrm{eff}}E_q^{\mathrm{THz}}(\tau).\tag{12}\]

The proportionality constant

\[K_{\mathrm{EO}}=\frac{2\pi L}{\lambda_0}n_o^3r_{\mathrm{eff}}\tag{13}\]

converts electric field into optical phase. Equation (12) is the simplest mathematical statement of field-resolved EO sampling.

4.3 Finite probe duration

Let \(g(t)\) be the normalized probe-intensity gate, \(\int g(t)dt=1\). The measured retardation becomes a temporal correlation:

\[\Gamma_{\mathrm g}(\tau)=K_{\mathrm{EO}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}E_q(t)g(t-\tau)\,dt.\tag{14}\]

For a Gaussian probe intensity with full width at half maximum \(\tau_p\),

\[g(t)\propto e^{-4\ln2\,t^2/\tau_p^2},\qquad \widetilde g(\omega)=e^{-\omega^2\tau_p^2/(16\ln2)}.\tag{15}\]

The finite probe pulse is therefore a Gaussian low-pass filter in angular frequency.

5. Balanced polarimetric readout

After the EO crystal, a quarter-wave plate and polarization analyzer convert the THz-induced retardation into an intensity difference. For ideal visibility \(V=1\) and zero bias, the two balanced channels may be written

\[I_{1,2}(\tau)=\frac{I_0}{2}\left[1\pm \sin\Gamma(\tau)\right].\tag{16}\]

The normalized difference is

\[D(\tau)=\frac{I_1-I_2}{I_1+I_2}=V\sin\!\left[\Gamma(\tau)+\Gamma_b\right],\tag{17}\]

where \(\Gamma_b\) represents residual static birefringence or analyzer bias. In the small-signal, properly balanced limit,

\[|\Gamma|\ll1,\ \Gamma_b\simeq0:\qquad D(\tau)\simeq V K_{\mathrm{EO}}E_q(\tau).\tag{18}\]

Outside the linear regime, the ideal thin-crystal inversion is \(E_q=\arcsin(D/V)/K_{\mathrm{EO}}\), subject to branch ambiguity and the non-ideal transfer function discussed next.

6. Complete electro-optic transfer function

A broadband THz component accumulates phase while the optical probe propagates at group velocity \(v_g=c/n_g\). Let the THz field inside the crystal be

\[\widetilde E(z,\omega)=t_{\mathrm{THz}}(\omega)\widetilde E_{\mathrm{in}}(\omega) \exp\!\left[i k_{\mathrm{THz}}(\omega)z-\frac{\alpha(\omega)z}{2}\right].\tag{19}\]

The phase mismatch between the THz phase and the moving optical gate is

\[\Delta k(\omega)=k_{\mathrm{THz}}(\omega)-\frac{\omega}{v_g} =\frac{\omega}{c}\left[n_{\mathrm{THz}}(\omega)-n_g\right].\tag{20}\]

The normalized coherent interaction factor is the longitudinal integral

\[G(\omega)=\frac{1}{L}\int_0^L \exp\!\left[-\frac{\alpha z}{2}+i\Delta k z\right]dz =\frac{1-e^{-[\alpha/2-i\Delta k]L}}{L[\alpha/2-i\Delta k]}.\tag{21}\]

For negligible absorption,

\[G(\omega)=e^{i\Delta kL/2}\operatorname{sinc}\!\left(\frac{\Delta kL}{2}\right), \qquad \operatorname{sinc}(u)=\frac{\sin u}{u}.\tag{22}\]

The complete small-signal detector model can then be written

\[\widetilde D(\omega)=H_{\mathrm{EO}}(\omega)\widetilde E_{\mathrm{in}}(\omega)+\widetilde N(\omega),\tag{23}\] \[H_{\mathrm{EO}}(\omega)=C\,t_{\mathrm{THz}}(\omega)\,r_{\mathrm{eff}}(\omega)\, \widetilde g(\omega)\,G(\omega),\tag{24}\]

where \(C\) includes optical intensity, analyzer visibility, \(2\pi n_o^3L/\lambda_0\), and calibration constants. The measured waveform is therefore not the bare incident field but

\[D(t)=h_{\mathrm{EO}}(t)*E_{\mathrm{in}}(t)+N(t),\qquad h_{\mathrm{EO}}=\mathcal F^{-1}\{H_{\mathrm{EO}}\}.\tag{25}\]
Physical interpretation
The probe duration limits temporal resolution; phase mismatch produces frequency-dependent cancellation through the crystal; THz absorption suppresses resonant bands; the EO coefficient may vary near phonon resonances; and interface transmission changes both amplitude and phase.

7. Step-by-step temporal evolution of the measured THz field

Define the delay-indexed field frame

\[F_n(x,y)=E_q(x,y,t_n),\qquad t_n=t_0+n\Delta t.\tag{26}\]

A delay scan generates an ordered sequence \(\{F_0,F_1,\ldots,F_{N-1}\}\). At a fixed pixel \((x_i,y_j)\), reading through the same sequence yields the local waveform \(E_q(x_i,y_j,t_n)\). Conversely, fixing \(n\) yields a two-dimensional snapshot of the field at one temporal slice.

7.1 Signed field versus intensity envelope

The temporal field changes sign on a sub-cycle scale, whereas the envelope is nonnegative. Therefore, the frame of maximum \(|E|\), the frame of maximum envelope, and the frame of maximum energy density need not coincide. Field-resolved detection preserves carrier polarity and phase; intensity detection does not.

7.2 Ideal sampling versus measured temporal evolution

\[F_n^{\mathrm{meas}}(x,y)=\int h_t(t_n-t')E_q^{\mathrm{true}}(x,y,t')\,dt'.\tag{27}\]

Thus, every measured frame is a temporally weighted average. A short gate and broad, flat \(H_{\mathrm{EO}}\) make \(h_t\) narrow and the frame approach an instantaneous field snapshot.

8. Analytic signal, envelope, phase, and arrival time

For a real time-domain field, the analytic signal is

\[\mathcal E(t)=E(t)+i\,\mathcal H\{E(t)\}=A(t)e^{i\varphi(t)},\tag{28}\]

where \(\mathcal H\) denotes the Hilbert transform, \(A(t)=|\mathcal E(t)|\) is the envelope, and \(\varphi(t)=\arg\mathcal E(t)\) is the instantaneous phase. The instantaneous angular frequency is

\[\omega_{\mathrm{inst}}(t)=-\frac{d\varphi}{dt}\tag{29}\]

for the inverse-transform convention of Eq. (1). Common definitions of local arrival time include

\[t_{\mathrm{pk}}(x,y)=\operatorname*{arg\,max}_t A(x,y,t),\tag{30}\] \[t_{\mathrm c}(x,y)=\frac{\int t\,A^2(x,y,t)\,dt}{\int A^2(x,y,t)\,dt}.\tag{31}\]

The peak time is intuitive but noise sensitive; the temporal centroid is more stable but can shift in the presence of echoes or long tails.

8.1 Group delay from spectral phase

Let \(\phi(x,y,\omega)=\arg\widetilde E(x,y,\omega)\). The frequency-resolved group delay is

\[\tau_g(x,y,\omega)=\frac{\partial \phi(x,y,\omega)}{\partial\omega}.\tag{32}\]

A robust arrival-time estimate can be obtained by fitting \(\phi\) with a straight line over a high-SNR frequency band. Phase unwrapping is required before differentiation.

9. From one temporal trace to the field cube \(E(x,y,t)\)

In two-dimensional EO imaging, each camera exposure provides a spatially sampled field projection at one delay. For pixel coordinates \((x_i,y_j)\), the data are

\[D_{ijn}=D(x_i,y_j,t_n),\qquad i=0\ldots N_x-1,\ j=0\ldots N_y-1,\ n=0\ldots N_t-1.\tag{33}\]

The measured data cube is a three-dimensional linear observation of the true field:

\[D(x,y,t)=\iiint h_x(x-x')h_y(y-y')h_t(t-t')E_q(x',y',t')\,dx'\,dy'\,dt'+N.\tag{34}\]

The temporal point-spread function is set by EO sampling; the spatial point-spread function is set by probe imaging, pixel integration, diffraction, crystal thickness, and any non-collinear geometry.

Temporal cut

Fix \((x_i,y_j)\): \(D_{ij}(t_n)\) is the local electric-field waveform.

Spatial frame

Fix \(t_n\): \(D_n(x_i,y_j)\) is the signed field distribution at one delay.

Space–time slice

Fix one coordinate, for example \(y=y_j\): \(D(x,t)\) reveals pulse-front curvature, tilt, diffraction, and propagation delay.

Vector data cube

Measure two independent projections to reconstruct \(E_x(x,y,t)\) and \(E_y(x,y,t)\).

10. Spatio-spectral reconstruction

At every pixel and for each measured field component \(q\in\{x,y\}\), the windowed discrete Fourier transform is

\[\widehat E_q(x_i,y_j,\omega_m)=\Delta t\sum_{n=0}^{N_t-1}w_nE_q(x_i,y_j,t_n)e^{+i2\pi mn/N_t},\qquad \omega_m=\frac{2\pi m}{N_t\Delta t}.\tag{35}\]

This produces complex maps of amplitude and phase:

\[A_q(x,y,\omega)=|\widehat E_q|,\qquad \phi_q(x,y,\omega)=\arg\widehat E_q.\tag{36}\]

The coherent spectral intensity is proportional to \(|\widehat E_x|^2+|\widehat E_y|^2\), with the proportionality depending on impedance and Fourier normalization. The spectral phase retains wavefront and arrival-time information that is absent from intensity-only maps.

10.1 Pulse-front tilt as a phase law

For a pulse with local delay \(\tau_p(x,y)\),

\[E(x,y,t)=a(x,y)u[t-\tau_p(x,y)]\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \widetilde E(x,y,\omega)=a(x,y)\widetilde u(\omega)e^{i\omega\tau_p(x,y)}.\tag{37}\]

If \(\tau_p=\tau_0+p_xx+p_yy\), then

\[\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x}=\omega p_x,\qquad \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial y}=\omega p_y.\tag{38}\]

Thus, a linear spectral-phase gradient across the beam is the direct signature of pulse-front tilt.

11. Propagation and space–time coupling

The angular-spectrum representation decomposes the spatiotemporal field into plane waves:

\[\widetilde E(k_x,k_y,z,\omega)=\widetilde E(k_x,k_y,0,\omega)e^{ik_z z},\tag{39}\] \[k_z(k_x,k_y,\omega)=\sqrt{\left[\frac{n(\omega)\omega}{c}\right]^2-k_x^2-k_y^2}.\tag{40}\]

The propagated field is recovered through the inverse transforms over \(k_x\), \(k_y\), and \(\omega\). Frequency dependence of \(k_z\) couples temporal and spatial evolution.

11.1 Dispersion expansion

Near a central frequency \(\omega_0\),

\[k_z(\omega)\simeq k_0+k_1(\omega-\omega_0)+\frac{k_2}{2}(\omega-\omega_0)^2+\frac{k_3}{6}(\omega-\omega_0)^3+\cdots.\tag{41}\]

The coefficient \(k_1=1/v_g\) produces delay; \(k_2\) produces group-delay dispersion and temporal broadening; higher orders distort the pulse asymmetrically. In a waveguide or structured beam, \(k_z\) also depends strongly on the transverse mode, so modal interference produces frequency-dependent spatial patterns and temporal beating.

11.2 Wavefront delay

If the spectral phase is decomposed as

\[\phi(x,y,\omega)=\phi_0(\omega)+k(\omega)W(x,y),\tag{42}\]

then the local delay is

\[\tau_g(x,y,\omega)=\phi_0'(\omega)+\frac{\partial k}{\partial\omega}W(x,y).\tag{43}\]

A curved wavefront \(W(x,y)\) therefore appears as a curved arrival-time map.

12. Vector field and frequency-resolved Stokes theory

Two orthogonal complex field components define the Jones vector at each spatial point and frequency:

\[\widetilde{\mathbf E}_\perp(x,y,\omega)= \begin{bmatrix}\widetilde E_x(x,y,\omega)\\[2pt]\widetilde E_y(x,y,\omega)\end{bmatrix}.\tag{44}\]

Using the convention \(S_3=2\operatorname{Im}(E_xE_y^*)\), the coherent Stokes parameters are

\[S_0=|E_x|^2+|E_y|^2,\qquad S_1=|E_x|^2-|E_y|^2,\tag{45}\] \[S_2=2\operatorname{Re}(E_xE_y^*),\qquad S_3=2\operatorname{Im}(E_xE_y^*).\tag{46}\]

The normalized parameters are \(s_i=S_i/S_0\). The degree of polarization is

\[P=\frac{\sqrt{S_1^2+S_2^2+S_3^2}}{S_0}.\tag{47}\]

For the polarized component, the ellipse azimuth and ellipticity angle are

\[\psi=\frac12\operatorname{atan2}(S_2,S_1),\qquad \chi=\frac12\sin^{-1}\!\left(\frac{S_3}{PS_0}\right).\tag{48}\]

For a fully coherent deterministic field, \(P=1\). The ellipticity is \(\tan\chi=b/a\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are the signed major and minor semi-axes. The sign assigned to circular handedness depends on the Fourier, viewing-direction, and polarization conventions; it must be stated explicitly.

12.1 Temporal vector trajectory

In the time domain, the measured transverse field traces

\[\mathbf r(t)=E_x(t)\hat{\mathbf x}+E_y(t)\hat{\mathbf y}.\tag{49}\]

For a broadband pulse, this trajectory need not be a fixed ellipse because the relative amplitude and phase of \(E_x\) and \(E_y\) can vary with frequency. Frequency-resolved Stokes analysis isolates the polarization state of each spectral component.

13. The inverse problem and regularized field recovery

The measurement equation can be expressed compactly as

\[\mathbf d=\mathbf H\mathbf e+\mathbf n,\tag{50}\]

where \(\mathbf H\) contains the EO temporal response, spatial blur, pixel integration, analyzer projection, and calibration. Direct division by a small transfer function amplifies noise. A frequency-domain Tikhonov/Wiener-type estimator is

\[\widehat E_{\mathrm{est}}(\omega)= \frac{H_{\mathrm{EO}}^*(\omega)}{|H_{\mathrm{EO}}(\omega)|^2+\lambda(\omega)}\,\widehat D(\omega).\tag{51}\]

The regularization \(\lambda\) suppresses unstable inversion where the detector response is weak. In a Wiener interpretation, \(\lambda=S_N/S_E\) is the noise-to-signal power-spectral-density ratio.

Interpretation rule. A spectral feature observed where \(|H_{\mathrm{EO}}|\) is close to zero is not reliably recoverable, even when the displayed deconvolved amplitude appears large.

14. Resolution, timing jitter, noise, and uncertainty

14.1 Temporal resolution

The effective temporal response is not determined by the stage step alone. A useful variance model is

\[\sigma_{t,\mathrm{eff}}^2\simeq \sigma_{t,\mathrm{probe}}^2+ \sigma_{t,\mathrm{crystal}}^2+\sigma_{t,\mathrm{jitter}}^2+\sigma_{t,\mathrm{electronics}}^2.\tag{52}\]

The stage step controls sampling density, whereas the convolution width of \(h_{\mathrm{EO}}\) controls true temporal resolution.

14.2 Timing jitter

If the shot-to-shot delay error is Gaussian with standard deviation \(\sigma_\tau\), averaging multiplies the true spectrum by the characteristic function of the jitter distribution:

\[\langle\widetilde E_{\mathrm{meas}}(\omega)\rangle= \widetilde E_{\mathrm{true}}(\omega)e^{-\omega^2\sigma_\tau^2/2}.\tag{53}\]

Timing jitter therefore attenuates high frequencies even when the delay step satisfies Nyquist sampling.

14.3 Field uncertainty in the linear regime

From Eq. (18), if the normalized balanced signal has standard uncertainty \(\sigma_D\), then the ideal field uncertainty is

\[\sigma_E\simeq\frac{\sigma_D}{V|K_{\mathrm{EO}}|}.\tag{54}\]

In the frequency domain, deconvolution produces

\[\sigma_E(\omega)\propto\frac{\sigma_D(\omega)}{|H_{\mathrm{EO}}(\omega)|},\tag{55}\]

which explains why the usable bandwidth is normally smaller than the mathematical Nyquist bandwidth.

14.4 Spatial averaging

A square pixel of widths \(p_x,p_y\) integrates the field over its area, multiplying the spatial spectrum by

\[H_{\mathrm{pix}}(k_x,k_y)=\operatorname{sinc}\!\left(\frac{k_xp_x}{2}\right) \operatorname{sinc}\!\left(\frac{k_yp_y}{2}\right).\tag{56}\]

Fine spatial structure is therefore attenuated before sampling. Camera pixel spacing and optical resolution are distinct quantities, just as delay step and temporal resolution are distinct.

14.5 Reliability of normalized Stokes maps

Because \(s_i=S_i/S_0\), noise diverges as \(S_0\to0\). A physically meaningful mask must therefore exclude low-intensity pixels before interpreting \(s_1,s_2,s_3,\psi\), or \(\chi\). In particular, \(\langle|s_3|\rangle\) is not equal to \(|\langle s_3\rangle|\); the former measures the average magnitude of local circularity, whereas the latter can vanish through cancellation of opposite signs.

15. Compact theoretical chain

Move the retroreflector

\(x_n\mapsto t_n=t_0+2x_n/c\).

Gate the THz field

The probe samples a convolution of \(E_q(t)\) with the EO response.

Convert field to phase

The Pockels effect gives \(\Gamma\propto n^3r_{\mathrm{eff}}LE_q/\lambda_0\).

Convert phase to intensity difference

Balanced polarimetry gives \(D=V\sin(\Gamma+\Gamma_b)\).

Assemble temporal samples

The ordered sequence \(D(t_n)\) reconstructs the signed field transient.

Repeat over the camera plane

Each delay produces a field frame, and all frames form \(E_q(x,y,t)\).

Transform along time

The pixel-wise DFT produces complex \(\widetilde E_q(x,y,\omega)\).

Recover wavefront and polarization

Spectral phase yields delay/wavefront; two components yield Jones and Stokes maps.
